Why this exists

Business context & what the pipeline delivers

The business problem

The bank issues cash loans. Credit losses come from borrowers who stop paying. We need a default risk score at the point of application, so risky applications can be declined, repriced, or routed to manual review — before money goes out the door.

What "default" means here

A loan is labelled bad if it is ever 30+ days past due at month-on-book 6 (30dpd_6mob). This is the label definition already used by the upstream datamart's label store; observed bad rate ≈ 29%.

Key consequence: labels arrive late

We only learn whether a January application was good or bad 6 months later. The entire pipeline — training windows, monitoring cadence — is designed around this delay.

The choice

Three classmate datamarts (simulating other tech families) were available alongside my own Assignment 1 pipeline. I adopted the LAM NGUYEN THANH THAO datamart end-to-end — bronze → silver → gold, code unchanged — and built the ML pipeline as its downstream consumer.

Why this one

  • Point-in-time correctness built in: its gold layer joins each feature snapshot to labels exactly 6 months ahead (multi-snapshot join) — the hardest part of leakage-safe credit data, already done and documented.
  • ML-ready outputs: a single feature store (72 features after encoding: financial ratios, capped outliers, one-hot categoricals, clickstream) and a clean label store (30dpd_6mob).
  • Realistic org design: in a bank, the feature platform is owned by another team. Consuming it as-is, contract-style, mirrors production reality and keeps one source of truth.

Known trade-offs (documented, accepted)

  • Outlier caps & one-hot encoders in the gold layer are fitted on the full history — a mild contamination we tolerate as datamart consumers; all ML preprocessing (imputation, scaling) is refitted on the training window only.
  • Feature store covers applicants who took a loan (labelled population) — consistent for train, inference and monitoring.

Design decision #2 — temporal correctness

No leakage: every decision uses only what was knowable that day

The trap we designed against

A label for an application made in month m only exists at m+6. Training "as of Jan 2024" on Dec 2023 applications would silently use loan outcomes from Jun 2024 — temporal leakage that inflates offline metrics and collapses in production.

Guardrails implemented

  • Maturity rule: the Jan 2024 training run may only use application cohorts ≤ Jul 2023 (labels fully observed by Jan 2024).
  • Out-of-time selection: models are compared on the two most recent matured cohorts (Jun–Jul 2023), not a random split — mimicking how the model will actually be used: trained on the past, applied to the future.
  • No test contamination: imputer & scaler are fitted on the training split only, inside an sklearn Pipeline, then frozen into the artefact.
  • Target isolation: labels live in a separate gold store; features never include outcome columns.

Data windows (application cohorts)

Aug–Dec 2023 cohorts are deliberately unused for training: their labels had not matured by the Jan 2024 training date. Leaving them out is the cost of honesty — and the OOT AUC (0.80) matched production AUC (0.75–0.81), evidence the discipline paid off.

Result: offline selection metrics predicted live performance within ±0.05 AUC — the pipeline's numbers can be trusted for governance decisions.

Design decision #3 — the model

Train three candidates, let out-of-time data pick the winner

CandidateTrain AUCTest AUCOOT AUC ▼Verdict

Random Forest (300 trees)0.9870.7780.784overfits; weakest OOT

Selection rule: max OOT AUC. The boosted trees win on the random test split but lose out-of-time — exactly the gap OOT validation exists to expose. With ~2.5k training rows and 72 features, the regularised linear model generalises best across months.

Why we're comfortable shipping a linear model

  • Stable: train→OOT AUC drop of 0.01 vs 0.20+ for the tree models.
  • Interpretable coefficients — easier conversations with credit risk & compliance.
  • Cheap to retrain monthly; deterministic (fixed seed) and fully reproducible.

What gets registered in the model bank

  • model.pkl — frozen sklearn pipeline (imputer → scaler → model), feature list, PSI baseline (training score deciles).
  • metadata.json — train/OOT windows, row counts, all candidate metrics, selection rule, label definition, deployment date.

Versioning & rollback

Artefacts are immutable, named credit_model_<deploy-date>. Inference resolves "the active model" as the latest version deployed on/before the scoring date — so re-running history always uses the model that was live at the time, and rollback is just re-pointing to a previous version.

Audit-ready: every prediction row carries its model_version; every version carries the evidence for why it was chosen.

Monitoring results — performance

Discrimination holds at AUC 0.75–0.81 across all six cohorts

AUC / Gini by application cohort. Production AUC stays in 0.75–0.81, bracketing the 0.80 OOT estimate from selection, and never approaches the 0.70 alert line. Mild month-to-month wobble is consistent with ~500-application cohort sizes.

Decision metrics at the 0.5 threshold. Accuracy ≈ 0.76–0.77 and precision ≈ 0.58–0.70; recall sits at 0.40–0.53 — the conservative threshold favours precision (fewer good customers wrongly declined). Threshold tuning against approval-rate and loss targets is the natural next business conversation.

Read: six months after deployment, the model still ranks risk essentially as well as it did at selection time — no retrain trigger from performance.

Monitoring results — stability & calibration

Scores are stable (PSI ≤ 0.05) and track actual default rates

Population Stability Index of each cohort's score distribution vs the training baseline (decile bins). All six months sit at 0.01–0.05, far below the 0.10 watch and 0.25 alert thresholds — the applicant population the model sees is the population it was trained on.

Keeping it healthy

Model governance: clear thresholds, a refresh SOP, and a path to scale

Monitoring policy (automated monthly)

Calibration gap< 5pp5–10pp> 10pp

Status is written into the gold monitoring table by the pipeline itself — the audit trail is the data, not a wiki page.

Refresh SOP

  • Scheduled: retrain every 6 months on the expanded matured window; challenger must beat champion on OOT AUC to be promoted.
  • Triggered: any ALERT, or two consecutive WATCH months → immediate retrain + root-cause review with credit risk.
  • Promotion = new model-bank version; old versions retained for rollback and for replaying history.

Deployment options

  • Today — batch (chosen): monthly Airflow scoring into the gold table; matches the monthly application snapshot cadence and is the simplest thing that works.
  • Next — on-demand API: wrap the same model-bank artefact in a FastAPI service for real-time decisioning at application submit; the artefact + version contract already supports it.
  • Scale: swap LocalExecutor → Celery/Kubernetes executor; datamart already runs on Spark.

Roadmap

  • Threshold tuning with the business (approval rate vs expected loss).
  • Feature-level drift (CSI) on top of score-level PSI.
  • Champion–challenger shadow scoring before each promotion.
Bottom line: the pipeline doesn't just produce a model — it produces the evidence, controls and escape hatches needed to run one responsibly.
